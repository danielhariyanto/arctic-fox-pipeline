ARCTIC FOX PIPELINE

Alaska gas, delivered to Alaskans — now.
Public summary · Sponsored by Fairbanks Pipeline Company · April 2026

What is Arctic Fox?

Arctic Fox is a proposed in-state natural gas and natural gas liquids (NGL) pipeline. It would carry Alaska North Slope gas from Prudhoe Bay down to Fairbanks, North Pole, and Eielson — a 484-mile, 12-inch line, roadside along the Dalton, Elliott, and Richardson Highways. It is privately financed and right-sized to actual Interior demand.

Why this matters for Interior Alaskans

  • Interior Alaska pays among the highest delivered energy costs in the United States — current Interior gas tariff runs about $24.76/Mcf.
  • Heating-season propane runs roughly $3.96/gal; gasoline-equivalent NGLs run $4–$4.65/gal.
  • Arctic Fox is engineered to deliver dry gas at about $5.54/Mcf — roughly 78% lower than today's market.
  • Propane drops to about $2.05/gal; butanes and pentanes+ also fall sharply.
  • Estimated household savings on heat and electric: roughly $2,000 per year at the base case.
  • Estimated aggregate consumer savings: ~$415 million per year across the Interior corridor.

How it works

  • 484 miles of 12-inch line, API 5L X-65, roadside corridor.
  • Sized to about 19 Bcf/yr of base demand — scalable upward as more communities and customers join.
  • Treatment and NGL fractionation at the Fox plant; clean dry gas delivered into existing utility networks.
  • Same corridor as TAPS — existing access, existing communities, established Arctic engineering.

Frequently asked

Will this compete with the larger Alaska LNG project?
No. Arctic Fox is sized for the Interior. It serves a different market on a different timeline. The two address different needs.
Is this a state subsidy?
No. Arctic Fox is privately financed. The State of Alaska may take up to 7.2% equity in exchange for in-kind easements and permits — no cash from the state.
Where does it run?
Roadside along the Dalton, Elliott, and Richardson Highways — the same corridor as TAPS. Existing access roads, existing communities.
When will I see lower bills?
Service depends on commercial commitments. The pipeline is engineered for service in years, not decades. Rates are set at cost-of-service from day one — savings show up the moment gas starts flowing.

Trunk ladder — build to suit

Arctic Fox commits to a 19 Bcf/yr base case on a 14″ trunk (Prudhoe Bay → Livengood) plus a 10″ stem (Livengood → Fairbanks). The trunk is the one variable NBOS decides: the open season sets the final diameter, and we build to the volume that is actually nominated — no stranded steel. Base case runs a uniform 14" pipeline start to finish — 44.1 Bcf/yr capacity, 2.3x headroom over the 19 Bcf base. As NBOS nominations grow the load, the trunk (and stem) auto-step up the ladder (14/16/18"); the whole cost model reflows with them. Trunk load comes from upstream takers — data centers, behind-meter power generation, mines, and dedicated gas to flange the line south toward Anchorage; Fairbanks takers set the stem.
