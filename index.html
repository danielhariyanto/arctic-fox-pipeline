The Gap

Alaska produces gas at world scale. Alaskans pay world-import prices for it. Arctic Fox closes that gap.

The Problem

Interior Alaska energy

High cost, fragile supply — sitting on top of one of the largest gas reserves in North America.

  • Interior Alaskans pay among the highest delivered energy costs in the United States.
  • Reference utility tariff (IGU Schedule C): roughly $24.76/Mcf delivered.
  • Heating-season propane and refined fuels routinely run $3.50–$4.65/gal at the burner tip.
  • GVEA generation depends on imported diesel, naphtha, and aging coal — exposed to barge, rail, and global crude swings.
  • Alaska spends ~18.5% of GDP on energy. The U.S. average is 6–8%.
  • Meanwhile, ~35 trillion cubic feet of proven gas sits at Prudhoe Bay, 500 miles up the Dalton.
The Solution

Arctic Fox

A small-bore, fit-for-purpose, in-state pipeline — engineered for what the Interior actually needs.

  • Right-sized to known load (19 Bcf/yr) with headroom up to 51 Bcf/yr — not export-scale, not speculative.
  • Roadside alignment — Dalton / Elliott / Richardson corridor, where access already exists.
  • Carries dry gas plus NGLs (propane, butanes, pentanes+) — products Interior Alaskans already buy.
  • Treatment and fractionation at the Fox plant; clean dry gas delivered to GVEA, IGU, Eielson, Clear, Pogo, and future data centers.
  • Privately financed. No subsidy. No state guarantee.
  • Standard utility-style transportation earnings: 14% ROE, regulated rate base.
  • Open architecture for state, Native Corporation, and load-center equity participation.
The Route

Prudhoe Bay → Livengood → Fox

Through Atigun Pass and the Brooks Range, roadside along Alaska's existing highway corridor.

  • 418.2 mi — Prudhoe Bay (CGF) → Livengood
  • 65.98 mi — Livengood → Fox
  • 484.18 mi — Total primary line
  • 5″ parallel NGL service line where required
  • Roadside alignment along Dalton, Elliott, Richardson Highways
  • No on-line compression assumed — CGF discharge at 4,000 psia
  • Treatment / fractionation at Fox plant
  • Secondary distribution: GVEA, IGU, Eielson AFB, North Pole
Why Now

The window for a disciplined, in-state buildout is open

  • Steel and line-pipe markets have stabilized post-2022. Mid-cycle pricing favors disciplined staged procurement.
  • Hilcorp is now the primary North Slope gas counterparty after BP's 2020 divestiture — a single, motivated seller.
  • GVEA is actively seeking long-horizon firm fuel for its Interior generation fleet.
  • IGU is buying spot LNG trucked from Cook Inlet — a willing buyer of pipeline gas at a real discount.
  • Eielson AFB and DoD installations have been directed to harden energy supply for the Interior corridor.
  • Alaska Native regional and village corporations are positioned to take equity in infrastructure that lowers community energy cost.
  • The corridor is already built — TAPS, the Dalton, and existing utility ROW make the engineering case straightforward.

Public FAQ

What this means for Interior Alaskans

Will Arctic Fox compete with the larger Alaska LNG project?
No. Arctic Fox is sized for the Interior — about 19 Bcf/yr at base — and serves a different need on a different timeline. The two projects address different markets: in-state heating, power, and industrial demand vs. world-scale LNG export.
How much will it actually lower my heating and electric bill?
Delivered dry gas is about $5.54/Mcf vs. an Interior reference of $24.76/Mcf — roughly 78% lower. Propane drops from ~$3.96/gal to ~$2.05/gal. Per-household savings on heat and electric are estimated at roughly $2,000/yr at base case.
Who pays for it? Is this a state subsidy?
No state subsidy. Arctic Fox is privately developed by Fairbanks Pipeline Company, Inc., with debt and equity from private sources. The state can take up to 7.2% equity in exchange for in-kind easements and permits — no cash outlay.
Where will it run?
Roadside along the Dalton, Elliott, and Richardson Highways — Prudhoe Bay to Livengood (418 mi) and Livengood to Fox (66 mi). Same corridor as TAPS. Existing access, existing communities.
What about the environment?
Roadside alignment minimizes new ground disturbance. PHMSA strain-based design is standard for Arctic service. Full EIS, ADEC, ADNR, BLM, and PHMSA review will apply — the same regulatory framework that governs every other major Alaska pipeline.
How long until first gas?
Indicative path: 2026 commercial close, 2027 FEED and permits, 2027–28 procurement, 2028–30 construction. Service date depends on commercial commitments — but the pipeline is engineered to be in service in years, not decades.
